# Cup Radar — World Cup 2026 Concept, Seattle HQ, and MVP Build Plan

## Original Premise

The idea is to create a website for the 2026 World Cup that aggregates information from reputable sources into one organized place. The site should prioritize Seattle, where the user is located, while also tracking other host cities across North America. The site should help a soccer fan who has been out of the loop quickly understand:

- When the World Cup starts and ends.
- Which games are happening and where.
- What is happening globally in the tournament.
- What is happening locally in Seattle.
- What tickets may become available, especially late or same-day opportunities.
- Whether there are opportunistic travel possibilities to Canada, Mexico, or other U.S. host cities.
- What city activities, fan events, news, jerseys, and culture items are worth tracking.

The site should function as a one-stop dashboard for World Cup information, with real-time or frequently updated match tracking, scores, fixtures, group standings, news, city activity, and ticket opportunities.

---

## TL;DR

The strongest version of this is not just “a World Cup website.” It is a **World Cup opportunity dashboard** built around one question:

> **What should I pay attention to today — globally, in Seattle, and opportunistically for tickets/travel?**

Recommended product name:

# Cup Radar

**Tagline:** *Your live map of matches, moments, tickets, and city energy.*

Seattle should be the “home base” vertical, with other host cities treated as expandable opportunity zones. The site should combine match tracking, city activity, news, ticket watch, travel windows, local logistics, fan events, jersey/drop culture, and “today’s best moves.”

# 1. Core Concept Extraction

## Surface Framing

A one-stop website for tracking the 2026 World Cup: fixtures, scores, standings, news, tickets, jerseys, local events, and city-specific activities.

## Deeper Product/System Opportunity

This is really a **real-time personal command center for World Cup decision-making**.

Not just:

> “What’s happening?”

But:

> “What’s worth acting on today?”

That is the product magic.

The goal is not to recreate FIFA.com, ESPN, Ticketmaster, Reddit, Google News, Visit Seattle, and local event calendars. The goal is to **orchestrate them into a personal intelligence layer**.

## Hidden Insight

The World Cup is too big to follow passively.

The tournament has three simultaneous layers:

| Layer | What it means |
|---|---|
| Global tournament | Matches, groups, injuries, upsets, scores, knockout scenarios |
| Host-city energy | Events, traffic, fan zones, watch parties, neighborhood activity |
| Opportunistic personal moves | Late tickets, cheap flights, spontaneous trips, jersey drops, city-by-city openings |

Cup Radar should sit at the intersection of all three.

## Why It Is Interesting

The use case is specific and emotionally real: a long-time soccer fan who is currently out of the loop, living in one of the host cities, and wants a way to re-enter the tournament with leverage.

The site should do the ambient work: surface what matters, reduce friction, and help the user make timely moves.

Primary user:

> A soccer fan in Seattle who wants to follow the World Cup intelligently without manually checking 12 different sources.

Secondary users:

> Host-city residents, casual fans, travelers, sports-curious locals, and opportunistic fans looking for last-minute tickets or events.

## Primary Use Case

Open the site in the morning or before lunch and immediately know what matters today.

## Core User Loop

1. User opens **Today Mode**.
2. Site shows today’s matches, live scores, group implications, Seattle activity, ticket opportunities, and top news.
3. User clicks into Seattle, a match, or a ticket/travel opportunity.
4. User takes action: watches, buys, attends, saves, shares, or tracks.
5. User returns tomorrow because the dashboard keeps changing.

A simple MVP scoring model:

| Factor | Weight |
|---|---:|
| Match quality | 25% |
| Ticket price vs. baseline | 25% |
| Personal feasibility | 20% |
| Travel/lodging cost | 15% |
| City/event upside | 10% |
| Weather/logistics | 5% |

Example output:

> **USA vs. Australia — Seattle**  
> Opportunity Score: **91/100**  
> Why: Home city, noon kickoff, major team relevance, no flight needed, likely high energy.  
> Watch: Price drops within 48 hours of matchday.

# 10. Founder-Style One-Pager

## Cup Radar

### Overview

Cup Radar is a live World Cup intelligence dashboard for fans who want to follow the tournament without drowning in scattered information. It brings together matches, scores, standings, host-city activity, ticket opportunities, local logistics, news, jerseys, and fan culture into one organized, daily command center.

The first version prioritizes Seattle, turning the city into a deeply tracked World Cup home base while also surfacing opportunistic ticket and travel possibilities across other host cities.

### Problem

The 2026 World Cup is massive: three countries, 48 teams, 104 matches, multiple host cities, real-time news, ticket shifts, fan events, travel complexity, and local disruptions. A fan who wants to stay informed has to check FIFA, ESPN, local news, ticket platforms, city pages, social media, team accounts, travel tools, and event calendars.

That is too much friction.

### Solution

Cup Radar gives fans one place to answer the daily World Cup question:

> **What matters today, and what can I do about it?**

It organizes global tournament information and local host-city activity into clear dashboards: Today Mode, Seattle HQ, Match Tracker, Group Tracker, Ticket Radar, City Jump, Newsroom, and Culture Tracker.

### Why It Matters

The World Cup is not just a sports tournament. It is a temporary global operating system layered onto real cities. For fans in host cities, the opportunity is not only to watch matches — it is to move through the city differently, find events, jump on tickets, meet the moment, and participate.

### Differentiation

Most sports sites tell you scores. Most ticket sites sell you seats. Most city sites list events. Cup Radar connects those layers.

It is not just informational. It is situational.

It helps a fan decide:

> Watch? Go? Buy? Wait? Travel? Meet up? Avoid traffic? Jump on this?

### Vision

Cup Radar starts as a Seattle-first World Cup dashboard. Over time, it can expand into a reusable live-event intelligence platform for major sports, festivals, tournaments, and city-scale cultural moments.

### One-Line Summary

**Cup Radar helps fans track the World Cup, follow Seattle, and spot real-time ticket, travel, and city opportunities from one clean dashboard.**

- `cities.json`
- `tickets.json`
- `events.json`
- `news.json`
- `teams.json`
- `standings.json`
- `alerts.json`

Then automate later.
